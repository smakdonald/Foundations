    Section 1.1 Statements

    Subsection What is a Statement?

    ‚ÄúEach of our acts makes a statement as to our purpose.‚Äù
    ‚ÄïLeo Buscaglia
    When we prove theorems in mathematics, we are demonstrating the truth of certain statements. We therefore need to start our discussion of logic with a look at statements, and at how we recognize certain statements as true or false.

    Definition 1.1.

    A statement is anything we can say, write or otherwise express that is either true or false.

    Remark 1.2.

    We will be making two assumptions when dealing with statements: every statement is either true or false, and no statement is both true and false. The first of these assumptions, often referred to as the Law of the Excluded Middle (and known formally as bivalence), may seem innocuous enough, but in fact some mathematicians have chosen to work without this powerful axiom.

    Example 1.3. Statement Examples.

    • ‚ÄúThe sky is blue‚Äù is a statement, as it is either true or false.
    • ‚ÄúYour birthday is October 23rd‚Äù is a statement, as it is either your birtday or it isn't.
    • ‚ÄúThe mitochondria is the powerhouse of the cell‚Äù is a statement.
    • ‚ÄúDo a backflip‚Äù is not a statement, because it cannot be said to be either true or false.
    For something to be a statement, it has to be either true or false in principle; it does not matter whether we personally can verify its truth or falsity.

    Subsection Combining Statements

    ‚ÄúConjunction junction, what's your function?‚Äù
    ‚ÄïSchoolhouse Rock
    What makes statements valuable for our purposes is that there are a number of useful ways of forming new statements out of old ones. An analog to this would be the ways we have of combining numbers to get new ones, such as addition and multiplication; if we did not have these operations, then numbers would not be very interesting.
    In this section we will discuss five ways of forming new statements out of old ones, corresponding to the English expressions: and; or; not; if, then; if and only if.

    Definition 1.4.

    Let \(P\) and \(Q\) be statements. The conjunction of \(P\) and \(Q\text{,}\) which is denoted \(P \wedge Q\text{,}\) is the statement that, intuitively, is true if both \(P\) and \(Q\) are true, and is false otherwise. We read \(P \wedge Q\) as ‚Äú\(P\) and \(Q\text{.}\)‚Äù The precise definition of \(P \wedge Q\) is given by the ‚Äútruth table‚Äù

    Example 1.5.

    Let \(P =\) ‚Äúit is raining today,‚Äù and let \(Q =\) ‚Äúit is cold today.‚Äù The statement \(P \wedge Q\) would formally be ‚Äúit is raining today and it is cold today.‚Äù
    We could express the same idea more succinctly in English by saying ‚Äúit is raining and cold today.‚Äù In general, we will try to use statements that read well in English, as well as being logically correct.

    Warning 1.6.

    The colloquial use of the word ‚Äúand‚Äù differs from the mathematical usage stated above. The mathematical usage means the above truth table, and nothing else, while colloquially there are other meanings in addition to this one. One source of confusion involving the word ‚Äúand‚Äù that is well worth avoiding is the colloquial use of this word in the sense of ‚Äútherefore.‚Äù

    Definition 1.7.

    Let \(P\) and \(Q\) be statements. The disjunction of \(P\) and \(Q\text{,}\) which is denoted \(P \vee Q\text{,}\) is the statement that, intuitively, is true if either \(P\) is true or \(Q\) is true or both are true, and is false otherwise. We read \(P \vee Q\) as ‚Äú\(P\) or \(Q\text{.}\)‚Äù The precise definition of \(P \vee Q\) is given by the truth table

    Remark 1.8.

    The mathematical use of the word ‚Äúor‚Äù always means an inclusive ‚Äúor,‚Äù so that if ‚Äú\(P\) or \(Q\)‚Äù is true, then either \(P\) is true, or \(Q\) is true, or both \(P\) and \(Q\) are true.

    Example 1.9.

    A simple example of a disjunction is the statement ‚Äúmy car is red or it will rain today.‚Äù This statement has the form \(P \vee Q\text{,}\) where \(P =\) ‚Äúmy car is red,‚Äù and \(Q =\) ‚Äúit will rain today.‚Äù The truth of this statement implies that at least one of the statements ‚Äúmy car is red‚Äù or ‚Äúit will rain today‚Äù is true. The only thing not allowed is that both ‚Äúmy car is red‚Äù and ‚Äúit will rain today‚Äù are false.

    Definition 1.10.

    Let \(P\) and \(Q\) be statements. The negation of \(P\text{,}\) which is denoted \(¬¨P\text{,}\) is the statement that, intuitively, is true if \(P\) is false, and is false if \(P\) is true. We read \(¬¨P\) as ‚Äúnot \(P\text{.}\)‚Äù The precise definition of \(¬¨P\) is given in the truth table

    Definition 1.11.

    Let \(P\) and \(Q\) be statements. The conditional from \(P\) to \(Q\text{,}\) which is denoted \(P \to Q\text{,}\) is the statement that, intuitively, is true if it is never the case that \(P\) is true and \(Q\) is false. We read \(P \to Q\) as if ‚Äú\(P\) then \(Q\)‚Äù. The precise definition of P \to Q is given in the truth table

    Example 1.12.

    A simple example of a conditional statement is ‚Äúif it rains today, then I will see a movie this evening.‚Äù This statement has the form \(P \to Q\text{,}\) where \(P =\) ‚Äúit rains today,‚Äù and \(Q =\) ‚ÄúI will see a movie this evening.‚Äù The truth of this statement does not say that it is raining today, nor that I will see a movie this evening. It only says what will happen if it rains today, which is that I will see a movie this evening. If it does not rain, I still might see a movie this evening, or I might not; both of these possibilities would be consistent with the truth of the original statement ‚Äúif it rains today, then I will see a movie this evening.‚Äù

    Convention 1.13.

    There are a number of variations as to how to write the statement \(P \to Q\) in English. In addition to writing ‚Äúif \(P\) then \(Q\text{,}\)‚Äù we could just as well write any of the following:
    • If \(P\text{,}\) \(Q\text{;}\)
    • \(Q\) if \(P\text{;}\)
    • \(P\) only if \(Q\text{;}\)
    • \(Q\) provided that \(P\text{;}\)
    • Assuming that \(P\text{,}\) then \(Q\text{;}\)
    • \(Q\) given that \(P\text{;}\)
    • \(P\) is sufficient for \(Q\text{;}\)
    • \(Q\) is necessary for \(P\text{.}\)

    Definition 1.14.

    Let \(P\) and \(Q\) be statements. The biconditional from \(P\) to \(Q\text{,}\) which is denoted \(P ‚Üî Q\text{,}\) is the statement that, intuitively, is true if \(P\) and \(Q\) are both true or both false, and is false otherwise. We read \(P ‚Üî Q\) as ‚Äú\(P\) if and only if \(Q\text{.}\)‚Äù The phrase ‚Äúif and only if‚Äù is often abbreviated as ‚Äúiff.‚Äù The precise definition of \(P ‚Üî Q\) is given in the truth table

    Example 1.15.

    An example of a biconditional statement is ‚ÄúI will go for a walk if and only if Fred will join me.‚Äù This statement has the form \(P ‚Üî Q\text{,}\) where \(P =\) ‚ÄúI will go for a walk,‚Äù and \(Q =\) ‚ÄúFred will join me.‚Äù The truth of this statement does not say that I will go for a walk, or that Fred will join me. It says that either Fred will join me and I will go for a walk, or that neither of these things will happen. In other words, it could not be the case that Fred joins me and yet I do not go for a walk, and it also could not be the case that I go for a walk, and yet Fred has not joined me.

    Convention 1.16.

    There are some variations as to how to write the statement \(P ‚Üî Q\) in English. In addition to writing ‚Äú\(P\) if and only if \(Q\text{,}\)‚Äù it is common to write ‚Äú\(P\) is necessary and sufficient for \(Q\text{.}\)‚Äù

    Subsection Tautologies and Contradictions

    ‚ÄúDo I contradict myself? Very well, then, I contradict myself; I am large - I contain multitudes.‚Äù
    ‚ÄïWalt Whitman

    Definition 1.18.

    A tautology is a statement that is always true by logical necessity, regardless of whether the component statements are true or false, and regardless of what we happen to observe in the real world.

    Example 1.19.

    An example of a tautology is the statement ‚ÄúIrene has red hair or she does not have red hair.‚Äù It seems intuitively clear that this statement is a tautology, and we can verify this fact formally by using truth tables. Let \(P =\) ‚ÄúIrene has red hair.‚Äù Then our purported tautology is the statement \(P \vee ¬¨P\text{.}\) The truth table for this statement is We see in column \(3\) that the statement \(P \vee ¬¨P\) is always true, regardless of whether \(P\) is true or false. This fact tells us that \(P \vee ¬¨P\) is a tautology. In general, a statement is a tautology if, as verified using a truth table, it is always true, regardless of whether its component statements are true or false.

    Definition 1.20.

    A contradiction is a statement that is always false by logical necessity.

    Example 1.21.

    The statement ‚ÄúIrene has red hair and she does not have red hair‚Äù is a contradiction. In symbols this statement is \(P \wedge ¬¨P\text{,}\) and it has truth table The statement \(P \wedge ¬¨P\) is always false, regardless of whether \(P\) is true or false. In general, a statement is a contradiction if, as verified using a truth table, it is always false, regardless of whether its component statements are true or false.
