Example 1.19.

An example of a tautology is the statement “Irene has red hair or she does not have red hair.” It seems intuitively clear that this statement is a tautology, and we can verify this fact formally by using truth tables. Let \(P =\) “Irene has red hair.” Then our purported tautology is the statement \(P \vee ¬P\text{.}\) The truth table for this statement is We see in column \(3\) that the statement \(P \vee ¬P\) is always true, regardless of whether \(P\) is true or false. This fact tells us that \(P \vee ¬P\) is a tautology. In general, a statement is a tautology if, as verified using a truth table, it is always true, regardless of whether its component statements are true or false.
